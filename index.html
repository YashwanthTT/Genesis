India's Endangered Artistic Heritage

Discover the stories behind traditional art forms that are on the brink of extinction. Your appreciation and support can help keep these invaluable cultural treasures alive.

Rogan Art

A 400-year-old tradition from Kutch, Gujarat, Rogan art involves painting on fabric using a thick, vibrant paste made from boiled castor oil and natural pigments. The artist uses a metal stylus to create intricate, free-hand designs, often from memory. This art form is critically endangered, with only one family—the Khatris of Nirona—continuing the practice.

Status: Critically Endangered

Paitkar Painting

Known as the "scrolls of the Santhals," Paitkar is one of the oldest scroll painting traditions in Eastern India, originating from Jharkhand. These narrative scrolls are made using natural dyes on paper and are traditionally used in storytelling ceremonies and ballads. Due to a lack of awareness and market access, many Paitkar artists have been forced to abandon their craft for manual labor.

Status: Severely Endangered

Kaavi Art

A unique form of wall mural from Goa and the Konkan region, Kaavi art is created by etching intricate designs onto fresh lime plaster that has been coated with a red pigment made from laterite soil. Once common on temple and home walls, this architectural art form has been nearly wiped out by modern construction methods and a lack of documentation.

Status: Nearly Extinct

Tholpavakoothu

A ritualistic shadow puppet theatre from Kerala, using leather puppets to enact the Kamba Ramayana. Performed in temples over 7 to 21 nights, it's threatened by declining temple patronage and modern entertainment.

Status: Endangered

Chittara Art

A tribal floor and wall painting tradition by the Devaru women of Karnataka. Using natural pigments, it depicts geometric patterns and fertility symbols. It is critically endangered as younger generations migrate and abandon the practice.

Status: Critically Endangered

Toda Embroidery

An intricate needlework from the Toda tribe of the Nilgiri Hills, Tamil Nadu. Woven on white cloth with red and black threads, it features geometric motifs. With a very small tribal population, the art form is highly vulnerable.

Status: Endangered

Manjusha Painting

Dating back to the 7th century in Bhagalpur, Bihar, this art form uses only three colors (pink, yellow, green) and depicts folktales. It is the only art form in India displayed in a sequential, scroll-like format.

Status: Vulnerable

Dhokra Art

An ancient metal casting technique practiced for over 4,000 years by tribal communities in Odisha and West Bengal. The number of skilled artisans is declining, threatening this historic craft.

Status: Vulnerable

Parsi Embroidery

A beautiful needlework with multicultural influences, found on 'Gara' sarees. With the decline of the Parsi community and the rise of machine-made textiles, this intricate art form is at risk.

Status: Endangered

Cherial Scrolls

A stylized narrative art form from Telangana, painted in panels on long scrolls of cloth. These scrolls are used by storytellers to narrate myths and epics. The tradition is now practiced by only a handful of families.

Status: Critically Endangered

Kavad Art

A unique tradition from Rajasthan where stories are painted on a portable wooden temple with multiple folding doors. A storyteller, the 'Kavadiya Bhat', recites the tales as they open each panel. This blend of carpentry, painting, and narration is fading.

Status: Endangered

Sanjhi Art

The ancient art of paper cutting from Mathura, Uttar Pradesh. Intricate stencils depicting stories of Krishna are cut from paper and used for rangolis. The skill required for this delicate art is becoming increasingly rare.

Status: Vulnerable

Ganjifa Cards

An ancient art of creating hand-painted playing cards, once popular in the Mughal courts. The tradition survives in small pockets of Odisha and Nashik, but the complex skill is known to very few artists today.

Status: Endangered

Patola Weaving

A highly complex double-ikat weaving technique from Patan, Gujarat, where both warp and weft threads are dyed before weaving. A single Patola saree can take months to create, and the skill is held by only a few master weavers.

Status: Endangered

Warli Painting

A tribal art form from Maharashtra characterized by its simple, geometric shapes depicting scenes of daily life. While popular, the authentic, ritualistic form of Warli painting is being replaced by commercialized versions, endangering the original tradition.

Status: Vulnerable

Kalamkari

An ancient art of hand-painting or block-printing on cotton textiles from Andhra Pradesh. The traditional, free-flowing pen (kalam) style is becoming less common than the block-printed version.

Status: Vulnerable

Pattachitra

A cloth-based scroll painting tradition from Odisha and West Bengal, known for its intricate details and mythological narratives. The traditional process of preparing the canvas and natural colors is being abandoned for faster, modern materials.

Status: Vulnerable

Madhubani Painting

Originating from Bihar, this art form is known for its intricate patterns and vibrant colors. While it has gained global recognition, the authentic practice on walls and floors for rituals is declining.

Status: Vulnerable

Phad Painting

A scroll-painting tradition from Rajasthan, depicting the heroic tales of local deities. These large scrolls are traditionally used in all-night musical performances. The number of artists and performers has dwindled significantly.

Status: Endangered

Gond Painting

A vibrant tribal art from Madhya Pradesh, characterized by its use of dots and dashes to create intricate patterns. While commercially popular, the ritualistic context and traditional knowledge are being lost.

Status: Vulnerable
